Introduction

Today, over half of the America’s jobs fall under the classification “middle-skill” - work requiring experience or credentials beyond a high-school education, but not a four-year degree. While the prevalence of these jobs offers considerable economic opportunity, there remains a stark gap between the growing availability of these positions and the number of Americans qualified to fill them. Moreover, with middle-skill jobs projected to account for 40% of all new job growth in the United States by 2017, this “skills gap” is poised to increase even further, barring corrective action from policymakers and labor stakeholders.

Our analysis explores the widening American skills gap through the lens of a key cohort of economic actors: states. Using data aggregated from a range of federal agencies/institutions (Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, National Center for Education Statistics), we have identified a set of state behaviors that are indicative of how well each state’s labor force has kept pace with the market demand for middle-skilled labor. The nature of these attributes - including high spending per student for community college education, job training programs, and secondary-level vocational training - point to increased investment in workforce development and more comprehensive utilization of federal funding sources as potential policy levers that can be used to create and maintain a future-ready workforce.
